Motivation: The Quest for Optimal Asset Allocation

1. The Optimization Problem & Sharpe Ratio

In quantitative finance, the core objective of asset allocation is to discover an optimal capital weight vector, \( \mathbf{w} \), that maximizes risk-adjusted performance. Mathematically, this is formulated as maximizing the Sharpe Ratio, which essentially measures the excess return generated per unit of structural risk.

The objective function is defined as:

\( \max_{\mathbf{w}} SharpeRatio(\mathbf{w}) = \frac{Benefit(\mathbf{w})}{Risk(\mathbf{w})} = \frac{\mathbf{w}^T \boldsymbol{\mu} - R_f}{\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^T \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w}}} \)

subject to \( 1^T \mathbf{w} = 1, \mathbf{w} \ge 0 \)

Here, \( \boldsymbol{\mu} \) represents the expected return vector, \( \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \) is the covariance matrix quantifying structural risk, and \( R_f \) is the risk-free rate.

2. The Traditional Flaw: Market Noise & False Correlations

Classical portfolio theories (such as Markowitz's Mean-Variance Optimization) and certain hierarchical models heavily rely on plug-in estimators (\( \boldsymbol{\mu} \) and \( \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \)) derived from historical prices. However, financial markets are notoriously contaminated with market noise and false correlations.

This reliance makes traditional models extremely fragile: minor fluctuations or estimation errors lead to ill-conditioned covariance matrices, resulting in extreme, unstable weight allocations that fail to withstand real-world market volatility.

3. The Machine Learning Solution: Factor GCN

To overcome the interference of market noise in this optimization problem, we bridge Machine Learning and Financial Optimization by introducing the Factor GCN framework.

Instead of relying on pure historical prices, we construct the underlying market graph using a multi-factor model rooted in real economic logic. In this architecture, Factor GCN serves as an End-to-End Non-linear Solver. By leveraging spectral graph convolutions, it automatically extracts deep topological features among assets, effectively filtering out noise, and maps the market state directly to the optimal simplex \( \Delta^{N-1} \) for robust portfolio allocation.

Methodology: The Factor GCN Architecture

Prelude: Graph Laplacian as a Market Filter

Before diving into the architecture, it is crucial to understand the role of the Graph Laplacian in our framework. In spectral graph theory, the normalized Laplacian acts as a mathematical operator that captures the underlying manifold of the data. For financial markets, applying Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) means utilizing this Laplacian as a spatial low-pass filter. It allows true economic signals to propagate smoothly across fundamentally related assets while aggressively smoothing out high-frequency market noise and spurious volatility.

STAGE 1: Factor-Based Structural Graph

Instead of relying on noisy historical return correlations, we construct a market graph from long-term fundamental factors containing firm characteristics such as Market Capitalization, ROA, and Debt Ratio. The distance is computed via a Gaussian kernel:

\( A_{ij} = \exp\left(-\frac{||z_i - z_j||^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \)

We incorporate a self-loop mechanism \( \tilde{A} = A + I_N \) and compute the symmetric normalized graph Laplacian:

\( \tilde{L}_{sym} = \tilde{D}^{-1/2} \tilde{A} \tilde{D}^{-1/2} \)

Here, \( \tilde{D} \) is the Degree Matrix (a diagonal matrix where each entry represents the sum of edge weights connected to a node). Normalizing by \( \tilde{D} \) is essential to prevent numerical explosion during matrix multiplication. This resulting normalized graph acts as a structural filter, allowing short-term signals to propagate through fundamentally similar assets while suppressing noisy price correlations.

STAGE 2: Spectral Feature Fusion & Scoring

We introduce short-term momentum signals (\( X \)) and leverage the structural map (\( \tilde{L}_{sym} \)) as a spatial low-pass filter. To extract high-level representations, we apply a feature extraction matrix \( \Theta \) alongside a customized Sparse Activation mechanism:

\( H = SparseAct_K(\tilde{L}_{sym} X \Theta) \)

The \( SparseAct_K(\cdot) \) is a Top-K activation function that zeroes out all but the most promising K signals, maximizing the signal-to-noise ratio. Next, a Scoring Layer projects these hidden features into a 1-dimensional confidence score (Z-score) for each asset using the scoring weight matrix \( \Phi \):

\( \mathbf{z} = H \Phi \)

Finally, to satisfy the long-only constraint, these Z-scores are mapped onto a probability simplex using a Temperature-scaled Softmax function, yielding the portfolio weight vector \( \mathbf{w} \):

\( w_i = \frac{\exp(z_i / \tau)}{\sum_j \exp(z_j / \tau)} \)

Note on Learnable Parameters:

For readers new to Graph Neural Networks, it is vital to note that \( \Theta \) and \( \Phi \) are Learnable Parameters (Neural Network Weights). While initially randomized, they will be continuously optimized via Backpropagation in STAGE 3, learning to dynamically map market signals directly to the maximum Sharpe Ratio.

STAGE 3: End-to-End Optimization via Gradient Descent

The capital allocation vector \( \mathbf{w} \) generated at the end of STAGE 2 represents a simulated portfolio deployment based on the neural network's current parameter state. To evaluate whether this simulated allocation is robust against historical market realities, we must calculate an objective score—the Loss Function (\( \mathcal{L} \)). Instead of traditional quadratic programming, we directly utilize the negative Sharpe Ratio of the simulated portfolio as our loss function to guide the model:

\( \mathcal{L}(\Theta, \Phi) = -SharpeRatio(\mathbf{w}) = -\frac{\mathbf{w}^T \boldsymbol{\mu} - R_f}{\sqrt{\mathbf{w}^T \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w}}} \)

Where \( \boldsymbol{\mu} \) is the asset expected return vector, \( \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \) is the structural covariance matrix, and \( R_f \) is the risk-free rate. Minimizing this objective loss \( \mathcal{L} \) is mathematically identical to maximizing the risk-adjusted returns of our entire fund.

The Mechanics of Gradient Descent: To find the parameter configuration that minimizes this loss, the framework establishes a complex, high-dimensional error landscape over the parameter space. We employ Gradient Descent to iteratively optimize our weights. The gradient \( \nabla \mathcal{L} \) points in the direction of the steepest increase in error. By continuously stepping in the exact opposite direction (\( -\nabla \mathcal{L} \)) scaled by a learning rate \( \eta \), the network actively pushes its internal weights toward the minimum error state. To ensure stable mathematical convergence and prevent the network from overfitting to transient market noises, we enforce a strict optimization budget limiting the model to a maximum of 500 training epochs.

Multivariable Calculus & Backpropagation: To precisely compute how a microscopic adjustment in our early layers alters the final portfolio performance, gradients flow backward from the loss layer through the entire network architecture. Utilizing the Chain Rule of Multivariable Calculus, the automatic differentiation calculates the partial derivatives with respect to our learnable parameters—the scoring matrix \( \Phi \) and the GCN feature extraction matrix \( \Theta \)—as follows:

\( \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \Phi} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{w}} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{w}}{\partial \mathbf{z}} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{z}}{\partial \Phi} \)

\( \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \Theta} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{w}} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{w}}{\partial \mathbf{z}} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{z}}{\partial H} \cdot \frac{\partial H}{\partial \Theta} \)

Through this analytical backpropagation, the error discovered at the final portfolio level serves as an immediate correction signal. By updating \( \Phi \) and \( \Theta \) side-by-step, the model autonomously learns to bypass market noise and directly maps raw factor graphs to production-ready, highly stable investment weights.

Empirical Results & Out-of-Sample Backtesting

To rigorously evaluate Factor GCN's real-world robustness and its ability to filter market noise, we conducted an extensive out-of-sample backtest. The simulation period spans from January 2024 to May 2026, a timeframe strategically selected for its "slow grinds upward followed by sudden, sharp drawdowns."

  • Data Source & Universe: We sourced highly reliable fundamental and pricing data from the authoritative TEJ Pro database. Our investment universe consists of the Top 150 listed companies in Taiwan by market capitalization to ensure absolute liquidity.
  • Testing Period (Jan 2024 – May 2026): This specific timeframe was strategically selected as our Out-of-Sample period. It represents a highly complex market environment characterized by an overall bull market interspersed with "slow grinds upward followed by sudden, sharp drawdowns". This serves as an ultimate stress test for the model's downside protection and risk-adjusted capabilities.
  • Baselines: Market Index (0050.TW), Equal-Weight (1/N), and the traditional Hierarchical Risk Parity (HRP) model.
  • Concentration Constraint: Adhering to the core philosophy of asset allocation ("Don't put all your eggs in one basket"), we enforce a strict 15% maximum weight limit per single asset to ensure robust diversification and mitigate idiosyncratic risk.
  • Simulation Plans & Rebalancing: To isolate and measure the pure predictive power of the structural model, the backtest was conducted under a frictionless market assumption (excluding transaction costs and slippage), utilizing two distinct execution protocols:
    • Quarterly Rebalancing: Serves as our standard baseline evaluation to observe long-term structural allocation logic.
    • Monthly Rebalancing: Introduced specifically to rigorously test the model's stability and adaptability. Frequent rebalancing in a volatile market proves whether the GCN can effectively filter out short-term noise.

Generalization and Future Work

While the current Factor GCN framework has demonstrated significant out-of-sample robustness, its underlying architecture is designed as a universal, highly adaptable mathematical chassis. Our future research and development will focus on the following core dimensions:

1. Customizable Topology

The current structural graph relies on fundamental firm characteristics. However, the framework is intrinsically agnostic to the input data type. Portfolio managers can flexibly substitute the underlying nodes and edges to align with their specific mandates.

For instance, the Gaussian kernel can be reconstructed using ESG ratings, alternative data (e.g., NLP sentiment scores from financial news), or macroeconomic indicators across global equity markets.

2. Dynamic Graph Attention (GAT)

Presently, the normalized Graph Laplacian (\( \tilde{L}_{sym} \)) serves as a relatively static spatial filter. While effective in stable regimes, sudden macroeconomic shocks (Black Swan events) can cause immediate structural regime shifts where static relationships temporarily fail.

To address this, future iterations will integrate Graph Attention Networks (GAT). By deploying self-attention mechanisms, the network will learn to dynamically adjust edge weights between assets in real-time, maximizing adaptability during extreme market volatility.

3. Tactical Alpha & Short-Term Offensive Strategies

Empirical evidence from our dual-frequency backtests clearly indicates that Factor GCN's true alpha-generating competitive edge is unlocked under shorter trading intervals. The stark outperformance of the monthly protocol over the quarterly setup suggests that the model might be exceptionally potent at digesting fast-moving momentum signals.

Consequently, we plan to pivot future research toward short-term offensive trading styles (e.g., weekly or bi-weekly rebalancing). By incorporating high-frequency liquidity factors and order-book dynamics, the GCN can be optimized to act as an aggressive tactical solver, capitalizing on transient market anomalies before they decay.
